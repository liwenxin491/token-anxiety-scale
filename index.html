Construct definition

What is token anxiety?

Token anxiety refers to anticipatory worry, emotional discomfort, resource-monitoring behavior, and perceived loss of control that arise when users believe their AI usage resources may be insufficient for their task demands.

These resources may include tokens, messages, rate limits, context windows, premium requests, AI credits, subscription allowances, API budgets, or employer-provided AI access.

Research background

From digital anxiety to metered AI resources

Token anxiety is positioned at the intersection of situational anxiety, digital stress, technology dependence, and the emerging usage-based economics of generative AI.

1. Technology-related anxiety and situational threat

Psychological anxiety is often understood as an anticipatory response to potential threat, uncertainty, or loss of control. In digital contexts, that threat can involve the anticipated loss of communication, information access, convenience, productivity, or social participation. Work on nomophobia, especially the Nomophobia Questionnaire developed by Yildirim and Correia, offers an important precedent for measuring anxiety around digitally mediated access. [1]

2. From device availability to resource sufficiency

Low-battery anxiety extends this logic from device absence to resource insufficiency. The device may still be present, but its future usability is threatened by a declining resource. Prior work shows that battery level can shape user behavior, including whether users continue or abandon mobile video watching. [2]

3. Digital stress and workplace technology strain

Token anxiety also belongs to the broader family of digital stress and technostress constructs. The Digital Stressors Scale treats digital stress as a workplace-relevant response to stressors created by digital technology. [3]

4. Generative AI as a metered cognitive resource

Generative AI capability is increasingly mediated through tokens, credits, rate limits, context windows, premium requests, model-specific allowances, and API budgets. OpenAI, Anthropic, GitHub Copilot, and Gemini all expose users or builders to some form of usage-based limit, pricing, or allowance. [4] [5] [6] [7]

5. Research gap and contribution of TAS

Existing measures explain important parts of this phenomenon, but none directly measure the user experience of AI capability as a dynamic, metered, partially opaque, and economically consequential resource. TAS is proposed as a construct-specific scale for studying how AI users monitor, ration, conserve, substitute, worry about, and emotionally respond to bounded AI usage resources. [8]

01

Metered intelligence

Users are not only interacting with intelligence. They are interacting with intelligence as a bounded, priced, and sometimes dynamically allocated resource.

02

Black-box consumption

Long context, tool calls, agentic workflows, cached input, output length, and model choice can all affect usage in ways users may not easily predict.

03

Workflow interruption

Usage limits can interrupt work after a user has built context, momentum, and trust with the system.

04

Economic and organizational pressure

AI resources may be experienced as personal expense, workplace infrastructure, productivity advantage, or an unevenly distributed professional benefit.

Emerging insight

A two-sided resource pressure

Token anxiety may not be a one-directional “resource depletion” phenomenon. A token-specific pattern appears when users feel pressure because their allowance is about to reset while a large share remains unused.

Too little left

I may be interrupted, downgraded, delayed, or forced to pay more.

Too much left near reset

I may waste value, fail to “use my plan,” or miss a productive window.

This “use-it-or-lose-it pressure” is currently treated as an emerging module, not yet part of the core validated construct.

Validation plan

From research prototype to validated scale

Phase 1

Cognitive interviews

Interview AI users to test whether the construct, wording, and examples match lived experience.

Phase 2

Pilot survey and EFA

Examine item distributions, factor structure, reliability, redundancy, and platform-specific wording.

Phase 3

CFA and short form

Confirm the factor model in an independent sample and reduce the scale to a shorter validated form.

Phase 4

Validity evidence

Test relationships with digital stress, financial stress, workflow interruption, AI dependence, and actual usage-limit experiences.
