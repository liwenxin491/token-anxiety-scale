Construct definition

What is token anxiety?

Token anxiety refers to anticipatory worry, emotional discomfort, resource-monitoring behavior, and perceived loss of control that arise when users believe their AI usage resources may be insufficient for their task demands.

These resources may include tokens, messages, rate limits, context windows, premium requests, AI credits, subscription allowances, API budgets, or employer-provided AI access.

Research background

From digital anxiety to metered AI resources

Token anxiety is positioned at the intersection of situational anxiety, digital stress, technology dependence, and the emerging usage-based economics of generative AI.

1. Technology-related anxiety and situational threat

Psychological anxiety is often understood as an anticipatory response to potential threat, uncertainty, or loss of control. In digital contexts, that threat does not need to be physically dangerous; it can involve the anticipated loss of communication, information access, convenience, productivity, or social participation. Work on nomophobia, especially the Nomophobia Questionnaire developed by Yildirim and Correia, shows that anxiety around mobile technology can be decomposed into dimensions such as not being able to communicate, losing connectedness, not being able to access information, and giving up convenience. [1]

2. From device availability to resource sufficiency

Low-battery anxiety extends this logic from device absence to resource insufficiency. The device may still be present, but its future usability is threatened by a declining resource. Prior work on low-battery anxiety has shown that battery level can shape user behavior, including whether users continue or abandon mobile video watching. [2] This makes low-battery anxiety an important conceptual bridge for TAS: the central issue is not only whether a tool exists, but whether the user believes the resource behind the tool can sustain the intended activity.

3. Digital stress and workplace technology strain

Token anxiety also belongs to the broader family of digital stress and technostress constructs. The Digital Stressors Scale developed by Fischer, Reuter, and Riedl treats digital stress as a workplace-relevant response to perceived stressors created by digital technology. Their scale development process began with 138 candidate items across 15 digital stressors and was refined into 10 stressor categories and 50 items using card sorting, exploratory factor analysis, and structural modeling. [3] TAS adopts a similar premise: a new technological environment can produce new stressors that existing measures may not fully capture.

4. Generative AI as a metered cognitive resource

Generative AI introduces a resource structure that differs from mobile battery, internet access, or ordinary software availability. AI capability is increasingly mediated through tokens, credits, rate limits, context windows, premium requests, model-specific allowances, and API budgets. OpenAI describes API use as token-priced, with input, cached input, and output tokens priced differently. [4] Anthropic describes usage limits as a time-bounded conversation budget. [5] GitHub Copilot has moved toward AI Credits based on input, output, and cached token usage. [6] Gemini API pricing similarly uses token-based paid tiers, context caching, and rate limits. [7]

5. Research gap and contribution of TAS

Existing measures explain important parts of this phenomenon, but none directly measure the user experience of AI capability as a dynamic, metered, partially opaque, and economically consequential resource. TAS is therefore proposed as a construct-specific scale for studying how AI users monitor, ration, conserve, substitute, worry about, and emotionally respond to bounded AI usage resources. The current v0.1 scale follows scale-development recommendations that emphasize construct definition, item generation, content validity, pilot testing, factor analysis, and validation in independent samples. [8]

01

Metered intelligence

Users are not only interacting with intelligence. They are interacting with intelligence as a bounded, priced, and sometimes dynamically allocated resource.

02

Black-box consumption

Unlike battery life, AI usage can be difficult to predict. Long context, tool calls, agentic workflows, cached input, output length, and model choice can all affect resource use.

03

Workflow interruption

Usage limits can interrupt work after a user has built context, momentum, and trust with the system, making the limit feel like a break in cognitive continuity.

04

Economic and organizational pressure

Users may experience AI resources as personal expense, workplace infrastructure, productivity advantage, or an unevenly distributed professional benefit.

Emerging insight

A two-sided resource pressure

Token anxiety may not be a one-directional “resource depletion” phenomenon. A token-specific pattern appears when users feel pressure because their allowance is about to reset while a large share remains unused.

Too little left

I may be interrupted, downgraded, delayed, or forced to pay more.

Too much left near reset

I may waste value, fail to “use my plan,” or miss a productive window.

This “use-it-or-lose-it pressure” is currently treated as an emerging module, not yet part of the core validated construct.

Validation plan

From research prototype to validated scale

Phase 1

Cognitive interviews

Interview AI users to test whether the construct, wording, and examples match lived experience.

Phase 2

Pilot survey and EFA

Examine item distributions, factor structure, reliability, redundancy, and platform-specific wording.

Phase 3

CFA and short form

Confirm the factor model in an independent sample and reduce the scale to a shorter validated form.

Phase 4

Validity evidence

Test relationships with digital stress, financial stress, workflow interruption, AI dependence, and actual usage-limit experiences.
